## **Abstract:** 

Unsupervised Anomalous Sound Detection (ASD) is a crucial, cost-effective tool for predictive maintenance within the fourth industrial revolution. This project presents a highly competitive pipeline developed for the 2026 DCASE challenge. Combining top-tier strategies from prior entries, our system integrates fine-tuned Bidirectional Encoder representation from Audio Transformers (BEATs) and Self-Supervised Light-weight Audio Model (SSLAM) architectures alongside a custom Audio Encoder. The system utilizes generalized mean pooling (GeM) and an ensemble of machine learning classifiers. On the 2026 development set, our offline pipeline achieved an official score on the development set of **69.17%** (online: **66.81%** ), outperforming the 2025 first-place baseline. Significant findings indicate that the auxiliary task of channel prediction improves SSLAM performance, machine types lacking attribute information benefit from pseudo-labeling, and score-based fusion yields a substantial performance boost of up to 6.691 percentage points.

## **Introduction** 

The fourth industrial revolution is transforming manufacturing through the digitalisation of production systems. By integrating technologies such as the Internet of Things, Artificial Intelligence, and cloud computing directly into the production pipeline, advanced sensors and embedded software can retrieve vast amounts of operational data. This data provides valuable insights that improve factory reliability, efficiency, and quality [1]. 

A critical task within this digital transformation is the automatic detection of mechanical failures [2]. While traditional methods such as vibration analysis, thermal imaging, or manual inspection are effective, they often incur high costs and require complex sensor configurations. Consequently, Anomalous Sound Detection (ASD) has emerged as a crucial alternative due to the low cost and ease of deploying acoustic sensing systems. Furthermore, audio data capturing provides a distinct physical perspective compared to other sensors, offering a significant performance boost when integrated into multimodal monitoring systems [3] [2]. 

By continuously monitoring machine acoustics, an ASD system can track operational health, identify structural or mechanical deviations, and potentially pinpoint precisely where and when failures occur. Implementing these predictive analytics allows factories to avoid long, costly production shutdowns while simultaneously boosting workplace safety. A general overview of this workflow is visualised in Figure 1.1. 

**Figure 1.1:** General architectural overview of an ASD system workflow. Figure taken from [2] 

As shown in Figure 1.1, the generalized ASD pipeline consists of three core phases: 

- **Input:** The system captures an acoustic wave signal emitted by the machinery via a microphone configuration. 

- **ASD System:** The raw audio is fed into the analytical core for feature extraction and 

1 

acoustic characterisation. A model then processes the features to evaluate whether the signal deviates from learned normal behaviour. 

- **Output:** The system outputs a binary decision, tagging the operational state as either normal or anomalous. 

To advance research and address the real-world complexities of this field, the DCASE challenge provides standardized tasks and datasets for evaluating acoustic algorithms [4]. Specifically, Task 2 of the DCASE challenge focuses on unsupervised first-shot ASD on industrial machines from different domains. By providing operational audio data of machinery in both normal and anomalous scenarios from the _Source_ and _Target_ domains, the authors not only focus on anomaly detection but also on domain generalisation [2]. The source domain is the domain where 99% of the training data is, while the Target domain has only 1% of the training data [2]. The different domains could be different rooms or even the same room, with different background noise. 

The major difficulties with this challenge are distinguishing between changes in noise and changes in machine sound. But the challenge does not only lie in noise vs machine, as there are many sources of variation, such as: domain shift, intensity shift, and change in machine configuration. To successfully solve the challenge, a system needs to be robust to most variations while being highly sensitive to potentially subtle anomalies.

## **DCASE Challenge – task 2** 

The goal of this report is to propose a solution for the DCASE challenge, specifically for task 2: "First-Shot Unsupervised Anomalous Sound Detection for Machine Condition Monitoring". This section will introduce DCASE and review existing solutions to provide direction for the technical analysis. 

## **2.1 Description** 

Task 2 of the DCASE challenge centers on the "first-shot problem under the domain generalisation setting." The first-shot problem requires a system to detect anomalies in a newly introduced machine type upon its first encounter, entirely without hyperparameter tuning. This focus mirrors real-world factory dynamics, where machinery is frequently replaced or upgraded, and where immediate operational safety is required. Because industrial failures are rare, collecting extensive anomaly data for model tuning is highly infeasible compared to gathering normal operational audio. Recognising these practical constraints, the DCASE organisers established specific technical requirements for all proposed solutions [5].

## **2.2 Requirements** 

DCASE task 2 has a set of predefined requirements set by the organisers, which the solution must satisfy: 

1. **Train a model using only normal sound (unsupervised learning scenario).** Because anomalies rarely occur and are highly diverse in real-world factories, it can be difficult to collect exhaustive patterns of anomalous sounds. Therefore, the system must detect unknown types of anomalous sounds not present in the training data, a task known as Unsupervised Anomalous Sound Detection (UASD) [2]. 

2. **Detect anomalies regardless of domain shifts (domain generalization task).** In real-world cases, a machine’s operational state or environmental noise can change, causing domain shifts. Domain-generalization techniques can be useful for handling domain shifts that occur frequently or are hard to notice. In this task, the system is required to use domain-generalization techniques for handling these domain shifts [2]. 

5 

3. **Train a model for a completely new machine type.** 

   - For a completely new machine type, the hyperparameters of the trained model cannot be tuned. Therefore, the system should have the ability to train models without additional hyperparameter tuning [2]. 

4. **Train a model both with or without attribute information.** 

   - While additional attribute information can help enhance the detection performance, we cannot always obtain such information. Therefore, the system must work well both when attribute information is available and when it is not [2]. 

5. **Train a model with additional clean machine data or noise-only data (optional)** Although the primary training data consists of machine sounds recorded under noisy conditions, in some situations, it may be possible to collect clean machine data when the factory is idle or gather noise recordings when the machine itself is not running. Participants are free to incorporate these additional data sources to enhance the accuracy of their models [2]. 

An explanation of the requirements, along with their importance to the system, is provided below for clarity. 

## **Requirement 1: Unsupervised anomaly sound detection (UASD)** 

_What it is:_ The model should be trained only on normal sounds and still be able to classify anomalous scenarios by distinguishing learned normal sounds from deviations. 

_Why it matters:_ In real-world scenarios, anomalous sounds are typically edge cases that rarely occur, are unpredictable, and are highly diverse. Thus, collecting representative anomalous data is inherently infeasible. Furthermore, intentionally damaging expensive industrial machinery solely to record failure audio is financially prohibitive, making it difficult to acquire sufficient anomalous data even for basic testing. This requirement ensures that real-world deployment remains scalable across different machines, as the system relies solely on learning normal operating sounds. 

## **Requirement 2: Domain generalisation task** 

_What it is:_ The system should still be able to detect anomalies even though data distribution changes. These changes may occur due to several conditions, such as machine ageing, environmental noise, operating conditions, and sensor placement. Specifically DCASE introduces the _Source_ and _Target_ domains, where each machine has 1 % of its data in the _Target_ domain. The domain difference might represent a different room or an environmental change. 

_Why it matters:_ The sounds recorded by the operating machines in practice may vary over time or location due to factors such as changes in workload, wear and tear, or factory noise. These changes are called domain shifts and may severely degrade the performance of the ASD system if it cannot generalise efficiently. Domain generalisation techniques should therefore be built into the system to ensure stable anomaly detection across domains. 

6 

## **Requirement 3: Cross-machine generalisation** 

_What it is:_ Given a previously unseen machine type, the system should be able to train an anomaly-detection model without additional hyperparameter tuning. 

_Why it matters:_ In real factories, new machines are introduced frequently, and there may not be time or data available for manual hyperparameter tuning. Additionally tuning the hyperparameters for anomaly-detection requires anomalous data and turns this into a supervised problem instead of the intended unsupervised. The requirement, therefore, improves scalability, reproducibility, and usability by requiring fixed hyperparameters. 

## **Requirement 4: Attribute-agnostic learning** 

_What it is:_ The model should be able to function both with and without "attribute information". Attribute information refers to changes in the machine configuration; some machines have labelled attributes, and some do not. An example is the machine-type "fan", which has different speeds as shown in the list below, with the attributes marked: 

- /fan/train/section_00_source_train_normal_0000_ **spd_1** .wav 

- /fan/train/section_00_source_train_normal_0001_ **spd_3** .wav

_Why it matters:_ Attribute information about machines, such as operating mode or machine ID, may not always be available. Thus, the system should not depend on any attribute information, but be able to maintain good performance even when it is absent. 

## **Requirement 5: Optional use of auxiliary data** 

_What it is:_ The requirement allows participants to incorporate auxiliary data, such as clean recordings of both machines and noise, in addition to the official noisy training data. 

_Why it matters:_ Some scenarios enable factories to acquire clean recordings of machine operating sounds or environmental noise. Therefore, participants may use these recordings to help distinguish machine sounds from noise.

## **2.4 DCASE 2026 Task 2** 

In the 2026 edition of the DCASE task 2, some changes have been introduced compared to the 2025 edition. In 2025, noise robustness was encouraged by providing either clean machine 

10

## **3.1.3 Augmentation** 

Augmentation is a powerful tool to increase the number of training samples, but this case is somewhat more sensitive, as we are working with anomaly detection and audio. Augmentaion of normal data should still result in normal data, and there it is required to be careful to not blur the boundary between normal and anomaly. **Room Impulse Responses (RIRs)** can be convolved with samples to generate synthetic samples belonging to a diverse set of domains. **White Noise** is used to apply a random signal equally across all frequencies. Using white noise for augmentation ensures that all frequencies are affected equally [25].

## **3.2.1 BEATs** 

The Bidirectional Encoder representation from Audio Transformers (BEATs) is a self-supervised framework designed to extract high-level semantic representations from audio data [13]. BEATs introduces an iterative pre-training strategy centred on discrete label prediction. This approach is inspired by the human ability to recognise acoustic events by capturing semantic patterns, e.g. a dog barking, rather than focusing on raw time-frequency details [13]. 

## **Preprocessing** 

The BEATs model receives the raw audio signal and has its own preprocessing pipeline before training. In Figure 3.2 a depiction of this pipeline can be seen. After this the image of the log-mel spectrogram is divided into smaller patches, creating localised sets of spectral features [13].

## **3.2.2 SSLAM** 

Another model that also uses Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) is Self-Supervised Light-weight Audio Model (SSLAM) and is building on top of other existing audio SSL models [12] [27] [14]. ## **3.3 Auxiliary Task Objectives** 

The primary objective is to separate anomalies from normal sounds. However, we cannot directly train to separate them, as we only have acces to normal samples. Therefore, we need to train the model with an auxiliary task objective that will still improve its performance on the primary objective.

## **3.4.4 Autoencoder** 

Another method for finding anomalies is to use an Autoencoder (AE) to find a reconstruction error. An AE is a neural network architecture defined by an encoder, a latent space (bottleneck), and a decoder. The only purpose of an AE is to reconstruct the input from the compressed latent space. To use it for anomaly detection, an option is to make it reconstruct a sample or a representation of a sample. If the network has only been trained on normal samples, the idea is that it should be bad at reconstructing abnormal samples [36]. This is the idea behind the baseline model provided by DCASE [5].

## **4.2 Audio Encoder** 

This section evaluates an alternative training objective and architectural strategy aimed at refining the encoder’s latent space for anomalous sound detection. We investigate a **one-versus-all** training strategy coupled with a custom loss function inspired by DML described in subsection 3.3.2. 

Rather than utilizing a purely unsupervised approach, a dedicated encoder is trained for each specific machine type. By exposing the model to the entire dataset while supervising it to isolate its target machine, the encoder is compelled to learn highly discriminative features unique to that machine’s acoustic profile. Specifically, when training a model for a target machine type _a_ , all internal attributes, domains, and augmentations belonging to _a_ are mapped to unique, individual target sub-labels ( _a_ 1, _a_ 2, . . . , _ak_ ), whereas all other out-of-distribution machine types are assigned a baseline label of 0. 

To structure this embedding space, the modified DML objective aggregates samples that share the same target sub-label into tight, compact clusters. Concurrently, the objective generates a repulsive force that drives different target sub-clusters apart and repels the out-of-distribution machine types (label = 0), ensuring clear boundaries in the latent space. 

## **4.2.1 Architecture** 

The proposed model architecture, illustrated in Figure 4.9, processes multi-channel raw audio data and maps it into a structured latent space using a symmetric Autoencoder framework [36]. 

The input consists of three raw waveforms: two primary audio channels and a third residual channel calculated as their difference ( _xres_ = _xch_ 1 _− xch_ 2). The encoder comprises five sequential convolutional blocks ( `ConvBlock1d` ), each containing a 1D convolution, Batch Normalization, and a LeakyReLU activation. Following these convolutional layers, Global Average Pooling (GAP) collapses the temporal dimension before a final linear layer maps the features to the embedding vector. A unit-sphere normalization is applied to this embedding to project all latent representations onto a hypersphere, ensuring stable distance-based clustering. The decoder reconstructs the waveform from this latent bottleneck by reversing the temporal compression of the encoder via four `DeconvBlock1d` layers utilizing transposed convolutions, Batch Normalization, and LeakyReLU activations. A final transposed convolutional layer applies a `tanh` activation function to bound the reconstructed signal within the digital waveform limits of [ _−_ 1, 1]. Finally, a length correction step crops or zero-pads the output to precisely match the original input dimensions.

## **4.3.3 Drawback** 

In the experiments so far, rank normalization has been used because it provides robustness to outliers by mapping scores to their relative order [34]. This is what particularly stabilizes the GMM when it collapses due to overfitting or singular covariance estimation. However, a primary drawback of rank normalization is its inherently offline nature. For DCASE Challenges Task 2, the full dataset is known, making it possible to run offline. That said, in a real-time streaming environment where data arrives sequentially, it must be processed independently. 

To address this, another version of the score-based fusion is made using Z-scores for standardization. To maintain independence among the test samples, a z-score cannot be computed using the test samples’ mean and Standard Deviation (std). Instead, a calibration set is made to estimate the parameters for each method (see Section A.6). Furthermore, the second version uses only KNN, GWRP, and AE to prevent GMM from dominating the scores if it collapses. 

Both the offline (Rank normalization) and online (Z-score normalization) versions will be evaluated in the experimental summary, with all methods and models fused.

## **Limitations and Future Work** 

## **5.1 Fine-tuning of Pre-trained Models** 

This section evaluates a series of progressively refined fine-tuning strategies for pre-trained audio models on the 2026 DCASE dataset. The investigation focuses on three primary dimensions: optimization strategies, the adaptation of pre-trained representations, and the exploitation of two-channel spatial information. Each iteration of the pipeline builds upon empirical observations from previous configurations to incrementally improve anomaly detection performance. 

A fundamental challenge encountered during fine-tuning was the misalignment between the auxiliary training objectives and the primary anomaly detection metric. Despite this divergence, the BEATs model demonstrated a sustained, albeit oscillating, improvement trajectory across training epochs. Conversely, the SSLAM architecture exhibited severe volatility; it converged and began overfitting to the training objective significantly faster than BEATs, without demonstrating a consistent improvement trend in the primary target metric. 

## **5.1.1 Technical Pitfalls and Architectural Constraints** 

During the development of the fine-tuning pipeline, a critical regression emerged when shifting the validation objective from the auxiliary classification task to the primary DCASE metric. Following this modification, full fine-tuning of the SSLAM model consistently resulted in execution failures at the start of the validation phase. 

An investigation was conducted to isolate the root cause. Because the number of frozen layers should theoretically have no bearing on the validation routine, initial troubleshooting focused on the validation source code; however, no implementation errors were detected. While the precise mechanism remains unresolved, empirical evidence suggests this instability is tied to the implementation of the generalized mean pooling layer. Consequently, full fine-tuning was disabled for this specific architecture as a mitigating remedy. 

Additionally, geometric constraints within the latent space may have limited performance. In the current pipeline architecture, score calculation in a normalized embedding space is restricted exclusively to the auxiliary classification task. Extending this normalization to the validation and final anomaly-score calculations is a key area for architectural refinement. 

60 

## **5.1.2 Channel Selection and Pseudo-Labeling Strategies** 

In the third iteration of the pipeline, the SSLAM model was trained exclusively on the first audio channel. This constraint was based on the assumption that SSLAM would mirror the BEATs model, which achieved peak performance on the primary channel. However, as illustrated in Figure 4.8, the results suggest that training SSLAM on the residual signal may have been equally beneficial. This hypothesis is supported by the fourth pipeline iteration, in which the residual signal proved highly effective at extracting features from the two-channel input. Consequently, expanding SSLAM V3 training to the residual signal remains an avenue for future work. 

Finally, evidence from the Audio Encoder experiments indicated that generating pseudo-labels for machine types lacking explicit attributes successfully enhanced performance. Implementing a similar pseudo-labeling framework for the pre-trained BEATs and SSLAM models offers a clear path toward maximizing representation learning in future iterations. 

## **5.2 Audio Encoder** 

The primary objective of the Audio encoder was not to compete directly with fully fine-tuned models, but rather to investigate an alternative training objective and strategy. Observations during training indicate a strong correlation between the primary and auxiliary tasks, specifically, the AUC score tended to decrease as the validation loss decreased. This relationship demonstrated that the training objective and strategy were worth investigating, even if the current overall performance does not match that of the fine-tuned BEATs and SSLAM models. 

The experimental results suggest that machine types without attributes achieve higher performance when trained with pseudo-labels. While the global performance remains below the finetuned baselines, the model achieves the highest performance in three specific areas: the AUC and pAUC for "Fan", and the pAUC for "BearingEmu". This demonstrates that the architecture excels at capturing certain specific sound characteristics. We believe that the model excels with machine types where the sound is relatively constant, thanks to its long context window. Based on these findings, we speculate that overall performance could be significantly improved with further machine-specific fine-tuning; however, the project’s timeline constraints did not allow us to explore this possibility. 

The primary limitation of this exploration is the time constraints, which limited further validation and testing of the correlation between primary and auxiliary objectives across different model architectures. Another drawback of this approach is the machine-specific strategy, which requires training a separate model for each machine type. It remains inconclusive whether machine-specific models inherently perform better, though the dominant results observed for “Fan” do support that possibility. 

Consequently, future work should include a comprehensive evaluation to validate the efficacy of this one-versus-all strategy. Furthermore, the proposed training objective should be evaluated 

61 

on alternative architectures, such as BEATs. Finally, the current model setup would benefit from extensive hyperparameter tuning tailored to each machine type to ensure optimal training configurations, potentially allowing it to match or exceed the performance of existing models.

## **Conclusion** 

This project demonstrates meaningful progress in the field of DCASE "First-Shot Unsupervised Anomalous Sound Detection for Machine Condition Monitoring". By combining strategies from the first- and second-place entries of the 2025 challenge [8] [10], we propose a system finetuning BEATs and SSLAM architectures, and further reduced uncertainty by constructing an ensemble of these fine-tuned models along with a custom-trained Audio Encoder. To extract robust representations, we applied spatial pooling with GeM to the embeddings of each audio sample, and subsequently used a combination of KNN, GMM, GWRP, and AE classifiers for anomaly detection. 

On the 2026 development set, our suggested **offline** pipeline achieved an official score of **69.17%** , whereas the **online** pipeline achieved **66.81%** . Compared with the 2025 first-place online system, which scored 61.6276% _±_ 0.0014%, our pipeline is a highly competitive contender. 

In conclusion, we have developed a pipeline suitable for submission to the 2026 DCASE challenge and have explored various alternative objectives and strategies. Our findings contribute to the ongoing discourse in this field and present promising opportunities for further research and innovation. Our auxiliary training task for predicting channels also improved the fine-tuned SSLAM performance, and our experimental results showed that machines without attribute information performed better when trained with pseudo-labels. 

Additionally, the investigation of score-based fusion, which combines multiple scoring methods, yields a substantial boost of 4.331 to 6.691 percentage points over the highest-scoring individual model (62.479%). The largest increase is in the offline pipeline using rank normalization; the other increase is in the offline pipeline as well.

## **A.6 Z-score parameters** 

To ensure the independence of each test sample while preventing any single method from dominating the aggregated results, the raw scores are normalized into Z-scores using their respective mean and std. The parameters for this normalization are derived from a calibration set, formed by partitioning the full training set—which consists of 990 source samples and 10 target samples—into 195 source samples and 5 target samples [5], while the remaining training samples are used to initialize each method. Calibration scores are then computed for the KNN, GWRP, and AE approaches, utilizing the three fine-tuned models detailed in subsection 4.1.4.
